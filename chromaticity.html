Step 0: Picking a Color Space

While the notions of chromaticity and gamut are general, every color space has a different gamut and the same light has different chromaticities in different color spaces. To be concrete, we will use a specific color space for this tutorial. Any color space will do, so we invite you to build your own and use it to drive the rest of the tutorial. Now toggle the "Show Chromaticities " switch. You will see a set of lines, each of which starts from the origin and goes through a spectral light. By simple geometry, we know that points on a line, while requiring different absolute amounts of RGB primaries (different coordinates in the RGB space), all have the same R:G:B ratio. This conclusion generalizes too any line, not just the lines that go through the locus.

How do a color that's mixed from 1:2:4 units of RGB primaries and a color that's mixed from a 2:4:8 units of the primaries relate? Well, recall from the previous tutorial that the amount of a primary is directly proportional to the power of that primary. So the second color can be obtained by doubling the power of each primary in the first color; similarly, halving the power of each primary in the second color gets us the first color. Intuitively, lights that have the same primary quantity ratio are said to have the same "objective color quality" while differing in the intensity.

More formally, we can calculate the primary ratio $r:g:b$ of a color and then normalize the ratio such that $r+g+b = 1$ (100%). The so-calculated $r, g, b$ values of a color are called the chromaticities of that color. Mathematically, the chromaticities of a color are calculated from its absolute quantity by:

$r = \frac{R}{R+G+B},~~~g = \frac{G}{R+G+B},~~~b = \frac{B}{R+G+B}$.

Each color now has an rgb triplet, representing the color's chromaticities. Similar to how we represent colors in the RGB space, we can also plot the colors using their chromaticities. The green curve in the chart above shows the chromaticities of the spectral lights. Hover over a chromaticity point and the corresponding RGB point on the same line; check the primary ratio in the tooltips and you can see that the primary ratio doesn't change when you plot a color using their chromacitities. Obviously, the exact chromaticity values of a color depend on the particular color space used to represent that color.

Knowing the chromaticities of a color tells us the inherent attribute of the color, and we can make that color brighter or darker by increasing or decreasing the amount of the primaries while keeping the chromaticities constant. In many practical color applications such as color photography and digital displays, chromaticities of a color is what we strive to capture (in the case of photography) or reproduce (in the case of display), and we are almost never be able to capture/reproduce the exact intensity of light in the scene (think about the power of the sun!).

Spin around the plot. Notice how all the spectral lights are now on the same plane. That's the natural implication of how chromaticities are defined: since we enforce $r+g+b=1$, all the colors in the chromaticity plot naturally lie on the $r+g+b=1$ plane. Toggle the "Show r+g+b=1 Plane" switch to convince yourself of that. If we were to define chromaticities such that $r+g+b=1.5$, all the colors will then lie on that plane. One way to think of the chromaticity plot is that we shoot rays from the origin to go through all the spectral lights, and then take a cut of all those lines with the $r+g+b=1$ plane. Mathematically, it is performing a perspective projection of the RGB colors onto the plane where the point of projection is the origin.

Chromaticity is inherently a 3D concept, but since the chromaticities of all colors lie on one plane, we can represent them in a 2D plot. The convention is to project the $r+g+b=1$ plane onto the r-g plane, and the resulting plot is called the rg-chromaciticy plot. You should try to spin the 3D plot around to project yourself, and you can also toggle the "Project" switch, which does the project for you.
